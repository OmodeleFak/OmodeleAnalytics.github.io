T-SQL: Dynamically Populating Multiple Transaction Tables in a Database

The purpose of this project was to dynamically populate the transaction tables of pharmacy store with several branches across Canada. Data sets used were synthetically generated to mimic real world scenarios where one or pharmacy assistants are attached to pharmacists. Logical and physical tables of relational database were created in appropriate cardinality and granularity with snowflakes schema. T-SQL scripts in Stored Procedures, which is efficient in querying cost, were written to automatically populate the transaction tables.

This satisfies the need of the business due its scalability and ability to accommodate changing parameters at minimal processing cost.

Cleaning and exploration of the Stop, Question and Frisk (SQF) Data with Python

The exercise here involved the exploration and cleaning of the SQF 2012 data sets with Python. With over 100 columns, the tasks here were conducted with select attributes. The process involved the use of Python libraries such as Folium, tqdm, openpyxl, pyproject, seaborn and many more. Some of the cleaning done include conversion of height to centimeter from feet, excluding unrealistic ages such as toddlers, removing some outliers and coercing a few others. And some of the explorations done include distribution check for age, weight, height and gender. Type of force applied, police identification and lot more were explored. The data sets can be assessed by clicking on the image.

Machine Learning: Predicting Titanic Survival with Various Classification Models

This project was aimed at predicting the likelihood survival in infamous Titanic event, which records the tragic events of April 15, 1912, when the ship sank, resulting in the loss of 1502 lives out of 2224 passengers and crew members. 5 classification models were employed with "Survived" as the label based on the key attributes including "Pclass", "Age", "SibSp", "Parch", "Fare", "Sex" and "Embarked".

While the accuracies were same for Random Forest (0.80), Support Vector Mechanism (0.80) and KNN (0.80), it was a little different with Logistic Regression (0.75) and Gradient Boosting (0.79). Within the context of survival prediction, precision and f1-scores are of greater importance to minimize false positives. Hence, Support Vector Mechanism with precision and f1-score of 83% and 74% respectively was found to the most suitable.
